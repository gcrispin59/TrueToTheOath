About the Initiative

The U.S. Marshals Service Civilian Special Deputation Initiative is an effort to identify and register qualified American citizens who may be called upon to assist the U.S. Marshals Service in executing its duties. This initiative allows the Marshals Service to maintain a database of willing and qualified individuals who can be deputized when necessary to enforce court orders and assist with other federal law enforcement functions.

Special Deputies work directly under the supervision of full-time U.S. Marshals and may be called upon during extraordinary circumstances when additional manpower is required to fulfill the Service's mission of enforcing federal court orders, protecting the federal judiciary, apprehending fugitives, and ensuring the integrity of the justice system.

Legal Authority

The authority for the U.S. Marshals Service to deputize civilians and other law enforcement personnel is established through several legal frameworks:

Judiciary Act of 1789

Historically, under Section 27 of the Judiciary Act of 1789, U.S. Marshals had the common law-based power to enlist any willing civilians as deputies for necessary assistance in the execution of their duties. This was commonly known as forming a "posse" in the western United States.

28 CFR ยง 0.112 - Special Deputation

The modern legal authority for special deputation is codified in the Code of Federal Regulations. The Director of the United States Marshals Service is authorized to deputize the following persons to perform the functions of a Deputy U.S. Marshal in any district designated by the Director:

  • Selected officers or employees of the Department of Justice
  • Selected federal, state, or local law enforcement officers whenever the law enforcement needs of the U.S. Marshals Service so require
  • Selected employees of private security companies in providing courtroom security for the Federal judiciary
  • Other persons designated by the Associate Attorney General

All such deputations expire on a date specified by the Director.

History of the U.S. Marshals Service

The United States Marshals Service is the nation's oldest federal law enforcement agency, established in 1789 during the first session of Congress. Throughout its history, the Marshals Service has served as the enforcement arm of the federal courts and has been responsible for carrying out all lawful orders issued by federal judges.

The Posse Comitatus Tradition

In the early days of American history, U.S. Marshals frequently relied on the "posse comitatus" (power of the county) doctrine to summon local citizens to assist in executing their duties. This practice was especially common in the Western frontier, where marshals often formed posses to apprehend outlaws and enforce federal law in remote areas.

Famous marshals like Wyatt Earp and Bass Reeves used their deputization authority to create posses that helped bring law and order to the American West. This historical precedent established the foundation for modern special deputations.

Evolution of Special Deputations

Over time, the nature of special deputations has evolved from informal posses to more structured programs. Today, special deputations are carefully regulated, with specific requirements for firearms training, background checks, and formal oaths of office.

Recent Examples of Deputations

Presidential Inaugurations

One of the most notable recent examples of large-scale deputations occurred in January 2021, when Chief Deputy U.S. Marshal Lamont Ruffin from D.C. District Court swore in approximately 2,000 National Guard troops as Special Deputy U.S. Marshals. This deputization enabled the National Guard to assist with security during the presidential inauguration, granting them certain law enforcement authorities they would not otherwise possess.

Similarly, in January 2025, approximately 380 soldiers from the Virginia and Pennsylvania Army National Guard were deputized by the U.S. Marshals Service in preparation for the 60th Presidential Inauguration.

Joint Law Enforcement Operations

The U.S. Marshals Service frequently leads multi-agency task forces where officers from various law enforcement agencies are deputized as Special Deputy U.S. Marshals. These task forces focus on fugitive apprehension, violent crime reduction, and other federal priorities.

For example, Operation North Star in 2022 involved the U.S. Marshals Service leading a collaborative effort with federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies in Washington, D.C., to apprehend violent fugitives. This operation resulted in over 170 arrests, including individuals wanted for homicide.
